## Lesson 1.1 · Welcome + How to Use This Course
**Runtime:** 8 min · **Format:** Talking head, seated
**On-screen workbook page:** Page 1 (Welcome + How to Use)

### Script

Hi. I'm Phyllis. Welcome to Purposefully Eating.

Before we go anywhere, I want to say something directly. Something I want you to hear before you watch a single other minute of this course.

You did not fail.

Whatever brought you here — whatever combination of diets, guilt, starting-over Mondays, mealtime stress, or late-night kitchen moments — none of it was evidence that something is wrong with you. It was evidence that the system you've been handed does not work. That's a very different conclusion. And where you take it matters.

Here's what this course is not. It is not a diet. It is not a macro plan. It is not a prescriptive eating framework that I'll ask you to follow for the rest of your life. If that's what you came for, I want to tell you now so you can make an informed choice.

Here's what this course is. It's six weeks of unlearning, and relearning. It's a framework for relating to food that is built on awareness, permission, and presence — not rules. And it's the method I've spent years teaching privately, now in a format you can take at your own pace.

Let me tell you how it's going to work.

You'll receive one module per week, for six weeks. Each module has four to five short lessons — about ten to fifteen minutes each. Plus a workbook, which I'll reference often. If you do one thing as soon as this lesson ends, let it be this: download the workbook. Print it if you can. Keep it somewhere visible.

In total, the course takes about 45 minutes of video per week, plus 15 to 30 minutes of workbook time. Less than a single TV episode. You are not signing up for a second job.

A few things to know about pacing. The modules drip-release weekly, but once unlocked, they're yours. You cannot fall behind. Some students move through quickly. Some take a year. Both are fine. There is no gold star for finishing fast.

One last thing before we begin. The invitation of this course is quiet. You may expect a dramatic transformation and receive, instead, something gentler — a gradual softening around food, a loosening of the grip, a Tuesday afternoon that passes without a single food thought. That is not a small thing. That is the whole thing.

When you're ready, move on to Lesson 1.2. I'll see you there.

### Lesson close / CTA

> *"Download the workbook before the next lesson. And take a breath. You're exactly where you need to be."*

## Lesson 1.2 · Why Diet Culture Fails (and Why You Haven't)
**Runtime:** 18 min · **Format:** Talking head, with slide overlays at key beats
**On-screen workbook page:** Page 3 (Cycle of Restriction diagram)

### Script

I want to tell you the truth about why diets don't work.

This is not going to be a villain speech about "Big Diet." It's going to be structural. The reason diets fail is a mechanism — a cycle — and once you can see it, you'll never un-see it.

Here is the cycle, in four parts.

It starts with restriction. You decide, with discipline and hope, to follow a plan. You cut something out. You measure something else. For a week, maybe two, maybe six, you follow it perfectly.

Then comes obsession. The food you've restricted — the cookies, the carbs, whatever — becomes louder in your mind. You think about it more. You notice it in every store, every Instagram reel, every commercial. Your brain, which is doing exactly what brains are designed to do around scarcity, is flagging that food as important. <em>More</em> important than it was before you restricted it.

Then comes rebellion. You break. It might be a small break — a "cheat meal." It might be a large one — a whole weekend. Either way, you eat the thing you've been thinking about. Often more of it than you wanted, because it feels like the last time before you start over Monday.

Then comes shame. You wake up the next day and feel like a failure. You promise to be better. You find a new plan. You start over.

And the cycle begins again.

I want you to hear me clearly: <em>this is not a character flaw</em>. This is not a willpower gap. This is what happens when any animal — human, rat, it doesn't matter — is subjected to restriction. The research is overwhelming. The cycle is a feature, not a bug. Diets produce this pattern. That is what they do.

So why do we keep trying them?

Because the diet industry makes more money when we fail. When you lose 15 pounds and regain them within a year — which 95% of dieters do, over a 2-to-5-year horizon — you come back for the next program. You buy the next book. You sign up for the next cleanse. The industry was engineered around your return. Your failure is the product.

This isn't cynicism. It's math.

Now — here's the shift I want you to make. I want you to hold two ideas at once.

One: you are not the problem.
Two: which means every time you "failed" a diet, you were actually correctly diagnosing that a broken system does not work. You were not weak. You were observant.

The real question, then, is not "how do I finally succeed at a diet." The real question is: <em>what would it look like to relate to food completely differently?</em>

That is what this course is about. And that is where we go next.

## Lesson 1.3 · Your Food Story — The Invisible Script
**Runtime:** 22 min · **Format:** Talking head, slower-paced
**On-screen workbook page:** Pages 5–8 (Food Story Timeline exercise)

### Script

Every one of us has a food story.

It was written for us, long before we had any say. By our parents, our grandparents, the adults at the tables where we first learned what eating meant. By the culture we grew up in. By a particular decade, a particular region, a particular family dynamic. By the mealtime that ended in a fight. By the grandmother who said "clean your plate." By the magazine on the coffee table when you were eleven with a tagline you still remember.

Your food story is not your opinion about food. It's the invisible script underneath every decision you make. And until you can see it — until you can write it down and read it back to yourself — you are going to be living it without knowing it.

So today, we're going to meet yours.

I want you to open your workbook to the Food Story Timeline. You'll find a set of prompts, and I'd like you to spend the next twenty to thirty minutes with them. Not right now — at some point today, or tomorrow, when you can sit with it.

The prompts will ask you to identify five key food memories. One from childhood. One from your teens. One from early adulthood. One from the last five years. And one from the last six months.

You don't need them to be dramatic memories. Some of yours will be. Some will be ordinary — a breakfast, a snack, a lunch your mom packed. The ordinary ones often carry as much information as the dramatic ones. Maybe more.

For each memory, I want you to write three things:
- What happened
- What was said about the food, or your body, in that moment
- What the adult in charge of that moment modeled

The third one is the most important. Not what you were told. What you saw. Because that's what you absorbed.

Now — a note about this exercise. Some of you are going to find grief here. Some of you are going to find shame. Some of you are going to find your mother, doing the best she could with what she had, and your heart may ache for her.

All of that is okay. This is not a blame exercise. We are not indicting anyone. We are mapping. We are naming. We are finding the script so we can read it back and decide what we want to keep and what we want to rewrite.

I'll give you an example from my own story.

When I was about seven, my grandmother made her famous peach cobbler. And I remember sitting at her table, being handed a slice — and I remember her saying, as she handed it to me, "well, a little won't kill you." And she laughed. And the slice was smaller than the ones she handed the boys. And I was a small, skinny little girl. I didn't need a smaller slice. I didn't need the comment.

I did not know, that day, that I had absorbed anything. But I had absorbed everything. A sentence about what would "kill you." A difference in serving size based on who was female. A laugh that told me the moment was fine, while also — not.

Forty years later, I could trace so much of my relationship with sweets back to that cobbler. Not because my grandmother was cruel. She was lovely. She was also a product of her own food story, which she was passing to me without knowing.

That is what we're doing today. Finding the moments. Writing them down. Beginning — only beginning — to see the script.

In the next lesson, we'll start to talk about what "Purposeful Eating" actually means — and how to write a new script over the old one.

## Lesson 1.4 · What Purposeful Eating Actually Means
**Runtime:** 15 min · **Format:** Talking head with concept slides
**On-screen workbook page:** Page 10 (The three pillars)

### Script

So what is this method, exactly?

Let me define it clearly. Purposeful Eating is a way of relating to food built on three pillars: <em>awareness, permission, and presence</em>.

Let me walk through each one briefly. You'll spend the rest of the course learning to practice them. Today, we're just naming them.

The first pillar is awareness. When you eat something, awareness asks: what was I feeling, what did my body need, how did this food feel in my body? Awareness is the practice of data collection — gentle, curious, without judgment. It replaces the old question of "was this food good or bad?" with the new question of "what was true for me?"

The second pillar is permission. Permission means that all foods are available to you. Always. Not as a "cheat day" exception. Not as a reward. As a baseline. When food is genuinely permitted, it loses its charge. The cookies in your pantry become, remarkably, just cookies.

The third pillar is presence. Presence is the practice of being there for the meal you're eating. Sitting down. Noticing the first bite. Knowing you're eating. So many of the eating moments I hear clients describe go like this: "I don't even remember tasting it." That's not a food problem. That's a presence problem.

When you practice all three — awareness, permission, presence — you're practicing Purposeful Eating. It is that simple and that hard.

Now, what Purposeful Eating is <em>not</em>.

It is not "eat whatever you want all the time." That's a misunderstanding I want to clear up early. Permission doesn't mean "anarchy." Permission means "no moral weight on the food." You still choose. You still decide. You still listen to your body. What changes is the frame. You are no longer eating within a system of good food and bad food. You are eating within a system of food and awareness.

It is not a weight loss program. Some students do lose weight over the course of this work. Some do not. Some gain a little. For all of them, the relationship with food becomes quieter, more peaceful, and less mentally expensive. That is the deliverable of this course. If weight loss is your only measure, this is not the right program for you.

And it is not intuitive eating, exactly — though it borrows from that tradition. Intuitive eating is a clinical framework with ten principles developed by Evelyn Tribole and Elyse Resch. Purposeful Eating is adjacent to that work but has a different emphasis — mindset-first, mother-forward, and built for real family life. If you've read their book, this will feel like a cousin.

Okay. Three pillars. Awareness, permission, presence. Open your workbook to the Three Pillars page and write them down. Say them out loud if you want. You're going to live with them for the next six weeks.

In Module 2, we're going to zoom in on the three shifts — the specific reframes that operationalize these pillars. That's where the real practice begins.

## Lesson 1.5 · Your Starting Point Assessment
**Runtime:** 12 min · **Format:** Talking head, interactive
**On-screen workbook page:** Pages 11–12 (10-question assessment)

### Script

We're going to end Module 1 with an honest photograph of where you are today.

This is not a test. There is no passing or failing. There is nothing to be ashamed of. This is a baseline — something you can look back at in six weeks and see what has shifted.

Open your workbook to page 11. You'll find ten questions, each rated on a scale of 1 to 10. I'm going to read them to you. Take a moment with each one. Rate yourself honestly.

Here are the questions.

<em>One.</em> How much mental energy does food take up in a given day? One means almost none. Ten means most of your thoughts.

<em>Two.</em> How peaceful is your relationship with eating, right now? One means completely at war. Ten means completely at ease.

<em>Three.</em> When you eat, how often are you actually present for the meal? One means rarely. Ten means almost always.

<em>Four.</em> How comfortable are you with all foods being available to you — with no moral hierarchy? One means very uncomfortable. Ten means fully at ease.

<em>Five.</em> How often do you eat emotionally — beyond hunger, in response to stress, boredom, sadness, or loneliness? One means constantly. Ten means rarely. <em>(Note — this one is reversed on the scale. Higher is better.)</em>

<em>Six.</em> How is your sleep? Unrelated to food, you might think. It isn't. One means terrible. Ten means deeply rested.

<em>Seven.</em> How confident are you in feeding your family in a way that aligns with your values? One means constantly second-guessing. Ten means grounded.

<em>Eight.</em> How does your body feel in a typical week? One means tired, foggy, disconnected. Ten means energized, clear, present.

<em>Nine.</em> How do you talk to yourself after a meal that felt "hard"? One means harshly. Ten means with compassion.

<em>Ten.</em> If a friend were watching your relationship with food for a month, how would they describe it? You can write a sentence here instead of a number.

Take fifteen minutes with this. Don't rush. Don't overthink. Write what feels true. Sign and date the bottom of the page.

Here's what happens next. We set this aside. You do the work of the next five modules. And at the end of Module 6, you retake this assessment — side by side with this version.

I don't want you to compare the two for any reason other than seeing what has moved. Some numbers will go up. Some won't. Some of the ones that matter most — like how you talk to yourself, how you feel in your body — will shift in ways you can't measure on a scale. The sentence in question ten is often where the biggest shifts live.

Take a breath. Fill out the page. Then move into Module 2 when you're ready. That's where we start to practice.

See you there.

### Workbook touchpoint
- Pages 11–12: Starting Point Assessment (10 questions, dated + signed)
