## Lesson 2.1 · Shift 1 — From Rules to Awareness
**Runtime:** 24 min · **Format:** Talking head + slide overlays for swap boxes
**On-screen workbook page:** Page 13 (Shift Tracker begins)

### Script

Welcome to Module 2. I've been looking forward to this one.

Here's what I want to say before we begin. The next three lessons are the three shifts. They are the core of everything. If you internalize nothing else from this course, let it be these three sentences. If you practice nothing else for the rest of your life, let it be these three movements.

Ready? Here's the first one.

<strong>Shift 1. From Rules to Awareness.</strong>

Let me show you what I mean.

Here's the old way. "Don't eat carbs after 6pm."

Here's the peaceful way. "How did I feel after I ate tonight? What does my body want tomorrow?"

Notice what's different.

The old way is a rule. It comes from outside of you. Some person — a nutritionist, a magazine, an Instagram account, a friend's friend — said this, and you accepted it. It has nothing to do with your actual body. Nothing to do with your actual life. And it breaks the first time something in your actual life contradicts it. You were up late. You were stressed. You had dinner at 8. The rule is broken. You now feel like a failure.

The peaceful way is a question. It comes from inside of you. It assumes your body has useful information and that your job is to listen. It can't "break" because there's nothing to break. It deepens every time you practice it.

This is the work of Shift 1. Replacing rules with awareness questions.

And it is quiet work. It doesn't produce dramatic weight loss on Instagram. It doesn't make you feel like you've "started something big." What it does is, over months and years, dissolve the architecture of diet culture inside your own mind. Until, eventually, you notice that you haven't followed a rule in a long time. You've been listening.

Here are three awareness questions to practice this week. You'll find them in your workbook on page 14.

Before your next meal, ask: <em>what is my body actually hungry for right now?</em>

During your next meal, ask: <em>what am I noticing as I eat?</em> Not "is this healthy." Not "how many bites." Just — noticing.

After your next meal, ask: <em>how do I feel now?</em> Hour one. Hour three. The next morning.

None of these questions demands an action. They are all listening questions. They are all data collection, without judgment.

You'll find something interesting happens when you practice these for a week. You stop caring about some of the things you used to care about. The question "how many calories" stops feeling relevant. The question "am I eating enough" becomes more interesting. The question "do I feel nourished" becomes the only question you're asking.

That's awareness displacing rules. That's Shift 1.

Now, the obvious pushback. "But Phyllis — don't I need rules? How will I know what to eat?"

My answer: you already know what to eat. You've always known. The rules have been drowning out the knowing. When you stop imposing rules, the knowing comes back. It takes a few weeks. But it comes back. Every single student I've worked with has had this experience. Trust it.

One more thing before you go. If you slip and impose a rule on yourself this week — we all do — do not punish yourself. Notice it. Name it. "Oh — I just made a rule. Okay." Then return to awareness. Rules made, noticed, and released are part of the practice. They are not a failure.

In Lesson 2.2, we'll do Shift 2 — which is the most counterintuitive of the three. I'll see you there.

## Lesson 2.2 · Shift 2 — From Restriction to Permission
**Runtime:** 22 min · **Format:** Talking head, warmer tone
**On-screen workbook page:** Page 15 (Permission Experiment)

### Script

Alright. Shift 2. This is the hardest one to trust — and the most powerful one to practice.

<strong>Shift 2. From Restriction to Permission.</strong>

Old way: "I can't have that."

Peaceful way: "I can have that. Do I want it right now?"

I need to tell you: almost every woman I've ever worked with, when I first introduce this shift, says some version of the same sentence.

"Phyllis, if I give myself permission to eat anything, I'll eat everything."

I want you to know I hear this. I've said it myself. I've believed it. And I want to tell you what I've seen — every single time, without exception — when a client actually practices permission for a few weeks.

<em>The opposite is what happens.</em>

Let me explain the mechanism, because understanding it matters.

When a food is forbidden — "I can't have cookies" — your brain files that food under a special category. Call it <em>scarce</em>. Your brain has survived for hundreds of thousands of years by tracking scarce resources very carefully. You will think about the forbidden food more. You will notice it in stores, on counters, at other people's tables. You will develop what feels like a craving, but is actually just scarcity-tracking. Your brain is doing exactly what it evolved to do.

Eventually — under stress, after a long day, at a party — you will break. You will eat the cookie. Often more cookies than you wanted. Because your brain has been telling you for weeks that this is the last opportunity. "Get them now, in case we run out." That's the real reason you overate. Not weakness. Scarcity signaling.

Now — what happens when you give yourself real, genuine, ongoing permission?

Within days — and I mean days, not weeks — your brain stops filing the food as scarce. It becomes available. Available foods are boring, in a way. They are not tracked. They are not noticed. They do not appear in every commercial. When the cookies in the pantry are "always available" — not as a reward, not as a cheat — they become, remarkably, just cookies. You might eat one. You might not. You might go three days without remembering they're there. That is what permission does.

I know this is counterintuitive. I know it feels risky. I want to give you a specific way to experiment with it.

Open your workbook to page 15. You'll find the Permission Experiment.

Here's the experiment. You pick ONE food — just one — that you've told yourself you "shouldn't" eat. Something you restrict, typically. Maybe it's a specific type of cookie. Maybe it's cheese. Maybe it's pasta. Pick something real.

You go to the store. You buy it. You put it somewhere visible in your kitchen. A countertop. A middle shelf. Somewhere you'll see it every day.

And then — you don't do anything. You let it sit. You live your life. You eat what you want when you want. You include it if you want. You don't if you don't.

You do this for at least a week. Two is better. Three is ideal.

What you will notice, by the end of the first week, is that your obsession with the food has quieted. By week two, you may go a day or two without thinking about it. By week three, you may realize you haven't opened the package.

Or — you may eat some. And that is also fine. Because eating a cookie under a permission frame is a fundamentally different experience than eating a cookie under a restriction-rebellion frame. You eat it. You enjoy it. You move on. There is no ledger. There is no shame. There is no promise to start over.

This experiment is the best single thing I can assign you in this whole course. If you do nothing else this week — do this.

A few things to address before I let you go.

One. Kids in the house. Yes, you can do this with kids in the house. You may worry the food will disappear into your 12-year-old's snack drawer. Unlikely. And if it happens, it happens. Buy another one. This is not about hoarding.

Two. Past eating disorders. If you have a history of a clinical eating disorder, please do this experiment with a therapist or dietitian — not alone. The experiment is not harmful. But permission-based work is a big shift for that nervous system, and it benefits from professional support.

Three. Your partner. You don't need their participation. Just keep your experiment food on your shelf. They do their thing. You do yours. Permission doesn't require buy-in.

I want you to leave this lesson knowing one thing.

<em>Permission isn't a free-for-all. It's the end of a war.</em>

That's Shift 2. Move on when you're ready.

## Lesson 2.3 · Shift 3 — From Shame to Curiosity
**Runtime:** 20 min · **Format:** Talking head, most intimate tone
**On-screen workbook page:** Page 17 (The Shame-to-Curiosity Reframe)

### Script

Shift 3. The quietest of the three. Possibly the most important.

<strong>Shift 3. From Shame to Curiosity.</strong>

Old way: "I can't believe I ate that. I have no self-control."

Peaceful way: "Interesting. What was I feeling before I ate? What was I needing?"

I want to tell you the difference between shame and curiosity in plain terms.

Shame says: <em>something is wrong with me.</em>

Curiosity says: <em>something is happening around me.</em>

Those two sentences produce completely different lives.

The woman who looks at an afternoon of eating with shame produces more eating. Because shame is itself a form of distress, and distress is a trigger for emotional eating. So the shame-reaction to the eating literally produces more eating. It's a feedback loop. And most of us live inside it for years, trying to shame ourselves into a different behavior. It does not work. It cannot work. The mechanism is adversarial.

The woman who looks at an afternoon of eating with curiosity produces insight. She asks the questions that actually matter. What was happening before I started eating? What had I been thinking about? What did I actually need, if it wasn't food? And — often — what am I supposed to do differently next time, based on what I'm learning?

That is the work. That is how change actually happens. Not through harshness. Through attention.

Now, a piece of hard truth. Shame feels, in the moment, like accountability. It feels like taking it seriously. Women are often praised for being "hard on themselves" — as if that's a virtue. Let me say this clearly: there is a meaningful difference between accountability and shame. Accountability says "I want to learn from this and do it differently." Shame says "I am bad and this proves it." One leads to growth. The other leads to the pantry at 9pm.

Here's the practice.

The next time you notice you've eaten emotionally — or eaten in a way that feels like it wasn't aligned with what your body was asking for — I want you to do three things. These are in your workbook on page 17.

<em>One.</em> Catch the shame thought. It will come. "I can't believe I did that." "Why do I keep doing this." "I have no control." Catch it. Write it down, verbatim, if you can.

<em>Two.</em> Ask three curiosity questions.

- What was I feeling 30 minutes to an hour before I started eating?
- What happened yesterday, or earlier today, that might still be in my body?
- What did I actually need, that wasn't food?

<em>Three.</em> Respond to yourself — out loud or on paper — the way you'd respond to a friend.

That last step is the one most women skip. They do the analytical work and skip the compassion work. The compassion work is what lands the shift. If you can't speak to yourself with warmth, the insight alone won't change the pattern. Practice the warmth.

I want to share something with you that will help. Nine times out of ten, when a client does this exercise, what emerges is not "I just wanted to eat." What emerges is: I was exhausted. I hadn't eaten enough at lunch. I'd had a hard conversation with my mother. I was lonely. My husband was traveling. My child got a concerning call from school. I was scrolling for an hour before I ate.

The eating was never the problem. The eating was the <em>message</em>. And when you receive the message with curiosity, you get to do something about it. You can rest. You can eat more at lunch tomorrow. You can call a friend. You can close your laptop.

When you receive the message with shame, you just get more shame. And more eating.

One more thing. There will be times you do this practice and no great insight emerges. Sometimes the trigger is just "I was tired and I wanted something sweet." That is not a failure of the practice. That is also information. Sometimes food is just food. The curiosity doesn't have to reveal something profound every time. Sometimes it just reveals a Tuesday.

That's Shift 3. In the next lesson, we'll integrate all three into a daily practice.

I'll see you there.

## Lesson 2.4 · Integration — Journaling the Shifts
**Runtime:** 15 min · **Format:** Talking head, practical
**On-screen workbook page:** Pages 19–20 (Daily Shift Journal)

### Script

You've learned the three shifts. Now we're going to build a tiny daily practice for living with them.

The practice is journaling. Two minutes a day. That's it.

I know. You have too much on your plate already. You don't need one more thing. Hear me out.

Two minutes. Not twenty. Not five. Two. You will set a timer. You will not think. You will write three sentences.

Here they are, printed in your workbook on page 19.

Sentence 1: <em>One shift I practiced today was ____.</em>

Sentence 2: <em>One shift I didn't practice today was ____.</em>

Sentence 3: <em>What I want to try tomorrow is ____.</em>

That's the whole journal.

Do it at the same time every day. I recommend the end of the day — before bed, after dinner, whenever your day softens. The consistency matters more than the timing. Pick a time. Stick with it.

Why this works. Writing is the difference between thinking about your practice and integrating your practice. When you write, you consolidate. Your brain files the information differently. You remember your day. You notice your patterns. And over weeks, you build a record of your own practice that no therapist or coach could give you — because it's yours.

A few things to know about this journal.

One. It is not a diary. Not a confession. Not a moral inventory. It is a 2-minute practice log. Three sentences. That is it.

Two. If you miss a day — or a week — return without drama. No apology. No "I have to catch up." Just pick up where you are. Consistency matters more than perfection.

Three. You do not need to share this with anyone. Not me. Not your partner. Not the course community. It is yours. Keep it private.

One practical note. The workbook has a full 30-day journal page set at pages 19 and 20. It's designed to be lightly marked. You can also use a separate notebook if you prefer. Whatever makes you most likely to actually do it.

Here is what I've seen happen, consistently, with clients who do this practice.

In week one, you feel silly doing it. You can barely remember what "shift" means. You write vague things.

By week two, you start to see patterns. "Oh, I always practice shame-to-curiosity on Tuesdays." "I never remember awareness at lunch."

By week three, you start to look forward to the two minutes. It becomes a small, private anchor in your day.

By week four, you have begun a life-long practice. The journal can stop. The awareness doesn't.

That's the point.

One last thing. If you want to do a longer version once a week — maybe Sundays, maybe Saturdays — feel free to do a "weekly review." Same three sentences, expanded. What shifts did I practice this week. What shifts did I miss. What do I want next week. That's optional. Two minutes a day is enough.

In Module 3, we're going to turn our attention to the body — rebuilding the hunger and fullness signals that diet culture trained you to ignore. I'll see you there.
